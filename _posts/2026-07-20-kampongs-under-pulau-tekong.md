Every enlisted man in Singapore has spent his first weeks of National Service marching through training areas called Selabin, Permatang, and San Yong Kong, without ever being told what those names actually mean. They aren't military codenames. They're the names of kampongs — real villages, with real families — that stood on Pulau Tekong until the island was cleared for the Singapore Armed Forces. The only thing left of them today is the geography recruits train on, still labelled with names nobody explains.

Tekong's settlement history goes back further than most people assume. Waves of migrants fleeing a civil war in Pahang arrived between 1857 and 1863, followed by a second wave between 1891 and 1895 during renewed conflict, founding villages like Kampong Pahang and Kampong Batu Koyok. Over the following century, the island filled out into a proper patchwork of kampongs — Selabin, Permatang, Pasir Merah, Unum, Sungei Belang, Ayer Samak, Pasir, Pengkalan Pakau, and more — home to a mixed community of Malays, Teochews, and Hakkas who fished and farmed the land. The population hit 4,169 in 1957 and kept climbing, reaching a peak of around 8,000 residents sometime in the 1980s. This wasn't a fishing outpost with a handful of families; it was a small town.

That town didn't survive the 1980s. Around 1987, the Singapore Armed Forces settled on Tekong as the single training ground for every enlisting Singaporean, and the island's civilian population had to go. Land surveyors moved through the kampongs assessing houses, crops, and livestock for compensation — and residents at the time felt the payouts fell well short of what their land and livelihoods were worth. There was no drawn-out legal fight or long public resistance recorded in the history; by the late 1980s, all of Tekong's villagers had been resettled onto the mainland, and the Basic Military Training Centre opened on the cleared island in 1999.

The only reason anyone still hears the villages' names today is bureaucratic convenience. When the SAF laid out its training areas on the newly cleared island, it kept the old kampong names as location labels — Selabin, Permatang, San Yong Kong — rather than invent new ones. So every year, tens of thousands of 18-year-olds spend their first nights of National Service in places named after villages that were emptied out to make room for them, with nothing on site to explain why.

Tekong isn't done being remade, either. In September 2025, Singapore completed its first-ever below-sea-level land reclamation — 800 hectares at the island's north-western tip, held back by a 10-kilometre coastal dike, built using a Dutch "polder" technique that uses roughly half the sand of conventional reclamation. The new land is earmarked for more military training space, freeing up room on the mainland for housing instead. Almost forty years after the kampongs were cleared, the island is still being physically reshaped for exactly the same purpose that erased them in the first place.

<div style="clear: both;"></div>

**Where it fits in the bigger story:** Pulau Tekong is one of the only places nearly every Singaporean man has personally set foot on, and almost none of them know it used to be someone's home — the clearest possible case of a place being hidden not by secrecy, but by nobody thinking to ask what a training area's name actually meant.
