For a lot of Singaporeans now in their 60s and 70s, going overseas was something that happened once or twice in a lifetime — a work posting, maybe a honeymoon, saved up for and planned around for months. Flying was priced for business travellers and the well-off, full-service and formal, the kind of trip you dressed up for. Today, the departure halls at Changi are full of people for whom none of that is true: students backpacking on a budget, retirees on their third cruise this year, whole families flying to Bangkok for a long weekend the way an earlier generation might have taken a bus to Malacca.

Two things happened at roughly the same time to make this possible. The first was money: median monthly income roughly tripled between 1996 and 2025, from about $1,950 to $5,775, so a trip that once meant months of saving became a more ordinary expense. The second, bigger factor was the arrival of budget airlines. Tiger Airways was incorporated in Singapore in December 2003 and began flying that September; Jetstar Asia launched the same December. Scoot followed in 2012, eventually absorbing Tiger Airways in 2017. Between them, and regional rivals like AirAsia, they broke the old model where an international ticket came bundled with a hot meal, a checked bag, and a fare that assumed you were flying for business. You can see both the SARS-driven dip in 2003 and this budget-airline effect on the chart — departures barely move until 2004, then climb almost every year after.

The other barrier that quietly disappeared was knowing what to do once you landed. Google Maps launched in February 2005; Google Translate followed in April 2006, with mobile apps arriving a few years after that. Before smartphones carried both in your pocket, independent travel meant a paper map, a phrasebook or a lot of hand gestures, and trusting a printed guidebook that might already be a year out of date. E-visas, mobile boarding passes, and instant currency conversion did the same thing to the logistics of a trip that budget airlines did to its cost — they took a list of small, specialised skills that used to matter and made most of them unnecessary.

The 2020–2021 collapse on the chart, when Singapore's borders shut for COVID-19, is really the exception that proves the point: once travel had become an assumed part of ordinary life rather than a rare event, its sudden absence felt like a genuine deprivation, not just a cancelled luxury. The recovery by 2023 — and the record set in 2025 — suggests that feeling stuck.

**Why it matters today:** the version of Singapore where an overseas trip was a once-in-a-lifetime event is, within living memory, still recent — which makes the packed departure halls at Changi a quieter marker of just how fast the island's relationship with the rest of the world has changed.
