Earlier this year, a 43-year-old man named Tang Bo Xiang was fined S$2,500 and handed a 12-hour Corrective Work Order — cleaning public areas in a bright vest, in full view of passersby — for flicking a cigarette butt onto the ground. It was his 13th littering conviction since 2000. Nobody outside Singapore has ever built a joke around the littering law. The one everybody knows is the chewing gum ban, which — decades after it made international headlines — has actually gotten gentler with time. The rules people abroad think are Singapore's strictest and the ones actually landing five-figure fines in 2026 turn out to be almost entirely different lists.

**The famous ones turned out to be the soft ones.** The chewing gum ban, in force since 1992, was never actually a ban on chewing gum — only on selling and importing it. Chewing a piece, or carrying some across the border for personal use, was never illegal, whatever the international reputation says. And the ban itself has already been partly rolled back: since 2004, as part of a US-Singapore free trade agreement, therapeutic and dental gum can be bought from a doctor or pharmacist. The other headline case, the 1970s ban on long hair for men, is in even worse shape as a scare story — it's simply gone. Long-haired visitors were once made to get a haircut at the airport or turned away at the border, and Led Zeppelin cancelled a gig rather than comply. But enforcement quietly faded through the late 1970s and 1980s, and the policy was formally lifted in the 1990s. It hasn't applied to anyone alive today for more than three decades.

**The boring ones are the opposite story.** Littering and failing to flush a public toilet both trace back to the same 1968 Environmental Public Health Act — the law passed to back Lee Kuan Yew's "Keep Singapore Clean" campaign — and neither has softened at all. Repeat littering offenders now face fines up to S$10,000 on top of a Corrective Work Order, and the National Environment Agency issued roughly 3,000 littering fines in the first three months of 2026 alone. Failing to flush a public toilet still carries a fine of up to S$1,000, and plainclothes NEA officers still do spot checks in hawker centres and other high-traffic toilets — no uniforms, no warning, just an on-the-spot fine if you're caught. Neither rule has ever needed a joke to stay enforced.

**Then there's the one genuinely serious rule that fits neither pattern.** Section 377A of the Penal Code, a colonial-era law from 1938 criminalising sex between men, sat unenforced for decades — Singapore's courts confirmed in 2022 it couldn't actually be used to prosecute consenting adults — before Parliament actually repealed it that November, effective from January 2023. It's the rare example of a dead-letter law that didn't just fade into irrelevance like the long hair ban; it was formally taken off the books, after sustained public pressure and years of the government insisting the status quo — unenforced, but still technically a crime — was itself the compromise.

What ties all five together isn't strictness or leniency — it's how unevenly Singapore's reputation tracks reality. The rules everyone's heard of are the ones the government has quietly relaxed or abandoned. The ones nobody talks about are the ones still generating fines and Corrective Work Orders every single week.

**Where it fits in the bigger story:** Singapore's "fine city" reputation was built on the two rules it has actually softened the most — a small irony that says less about Singapore than about how a joke, once it lands internationally, keeps circulating long after the reality underneath it has moved on.
