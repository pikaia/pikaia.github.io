Somewhere in Singapore right now, a 78-year-old is checking a family WhatsApp group before breakfast, sending a good-morning sticker to a chat of ex-colleagues, or reading updates from her Active Ageing Centre's exercise group — quietly maintaining a social circle that, a decade ago, would have shrunk along with her mobility. It's an unglamorous kind of connection, but it's doing real work: a government push to get seniors online has ended up rebuilding something closer to old kampong-style closeness than anyone quite planned for.

*A community club — the kind of place where Digital Ambassadors run in-person training sessions for seniors. (Photo: Terence Ong / Wikimedia Commons, CC BY-SA 3.0)*

The programme behind it is called Seniors Go Digital, launched in June 2020 as the pandemic made digital exclusion suddenly urgent. Community clubs had actually been trying versions of this for decades — Marine Parade set up Singapore's first CC computer club back in 1980 — but Seniors Go Digital scaled it into something else entirely: more than 1,000 Digital Ambassadors deployed islandwide, teaching seniors not just how to use Singpass or book a doctor's appointment, but how to message, video call, and join group chats. Over 190,000 seniors have been trained so far, up from about 36,000 in the programme's first year. A companion scheme, Mobile Access for Seniors, subsidised smartphones and data plans for more than 13,000 lower-income seniors who otherwise wouldn't have had a device to train on at all.

The problem it was built to solve is a real one: a Singapore study of nearly 6,000 seniors aged 68 to 77 found that 46.5% showed signs of social isolation. What's less obvious is that teaching someone to use a smartphone doesn't automatically fix that — a local trial found digital-literacy training alone often fell short, simply because a senior's existing friends and family weren't online either, so there was no one to actually talk to on the other end. The fix that worked, in a related programme called Project Wire Up, was explicitly three-part: hand over a smartphone, train the person to use it, and then deliberately reconnect them to their own social network — not just teach the skill, but rebuild the community around it. A separate Singapore study following that same logic found the mediation runs in a clear line: digital literacy leads to stronger social connectedness, which leads to less isolation, which shows up as measurably better wellbeing.

That's the quieter story behind all those senior citizens tapping away in coffee shops and community clubs — it was never really about the smartphone. It was about giving a generation that had started losing its social circles a new, unglamorous way to hold onto them.

**Why it matters today:** a programme built to close a "digital divide" ended up doing something more specific — rebuilding the kind of tight, everyday social contact that public housing and smaller families had quietly eroded, one group chat at a time.
