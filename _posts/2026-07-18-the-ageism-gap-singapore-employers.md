Somewhere in the CBD right now, a 61-year-old is putting together a resume for the first time in twenty years — not because she wants a hobby, but because her CPF payouts don't quite stretch to cover the month, and re-employment at her old company runs out at 68. On paper, the system is built for exactly this: government messaging, tripartite guidelines, and a formal re-employment law all say older workers are welcome to keep working. In practice, a lot of resumes like hers go nowhere, and the reasons why rarely make it into any official statement.

Singapore's retirement age was raised to 63 in 2022, with re-employment guaranteed to 68; it rises again to 64 from July 2026, on the way to a targeted 65 and 70 by 2030. The policy logic is straightforward — with a quarter of the resident workforce already 55 or older, and CPF savings for many of that generation built up under an earlier, less generous system, working longer isn't optional for a lot of people. Employment rates among seniors have risen accordingly: 70.8% of 55-to-64-year-olds and 31.5% of those 65 and over were working as of 2025, both up from a few years earlier.

The gap shows up once you ask employers directly. In a 2023 survey by NTUC, the Singapore University of Social Sciences, and the Tsao Foundation, 52% of employers said they'd likely hire a worker aged 55 or older — a respectable-sounding topline number. Split by whether the employer already had older workers on staff, though, the number falls apart:

An employer who already has older workers on the team is roughly three times more likely to say they'd hire another one than an employer who has none at all. That's not really a story about principled support for older workers — it's a story about familiarity, or the lack of it, doing most of the work behind the topline number. The employers who most need convincing are the ones the "52%" statistic hides.

The same gap turns up in complaint data. Between 2018 and 2022, the Tripartite Alliance for Fair and Progressive Employment Practices received an average of 77 age-related complaints a year, already the second most common category. By 2023, age discrimination had become the single most-cited form of workplace discrimination in Singapore, ahead of both race and gender. Among older jobseekers specifically, age is the top-cited barrier to getting hired at all.

Little of this looks like deliberate exclusion. Research on managers in this region points instead to unconscious assumptions: that older employees work more slowly, or that their skills — particularly around technology — have gone stale, even when neither is actually true for the person in front of them. It's a quieter kind of bias than an outright rejection, which is part of why it survives so easily alongside official messaging that says the opposite.

**Why it matters today:** Singapore has largely solved the policy side of ageism — the laws, the guidelines, the retirement-age math all point the same direction — but a law can mandate that a door stays open without doing anything about who actually decides to walk an older applicant through it, which is the part the statistics above are still measuring.
