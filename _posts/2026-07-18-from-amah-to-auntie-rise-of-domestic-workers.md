Before there were foreign domestic workers, there were amahs — Cantonese women, some of them majie who had taken vows never to marry, who ran Singaporean households through the mid-20th century. By the 1970s they were a vanishing breed: as female literacy rose from 27% in 1957 to over 60% by 1970, the young women who might once have gone into domestic service were going into factories and offices instead. Singapore's households still needed the help. It just had to come from somewhere else.

*Lucky Plaza on Orchard Road, photographed on a Sunday — since the late 1980s, a de facto gathering place for the city's Filipino domestic workers on their day off. (Photo: Exec8 / Wikimedia Commons, CC BY-SA 3.0)*

That "somewhere else" became a formal system in 1978, when Singapore introduced a scheme allowing households to sponsor domestic workers from the region on work permits — one of the first countries in the world to do so. Growth was slow at first: by some accounts there were only around 50,000 foreign domestic workers by 1990, enough for roughly one in every 13 households. What followed was decades of steady expansion, tracked in the official numbers from 2009 onward.

What the chart doesn't show is quite as interesting as what it does. Over this period, Singapore's total foreign workforce grew from about 1.05 million to 1.64 million — and foreign domestic workers have held fairly steady at somewhere between 16% and 20% of that total the whole way through. In other words, the number of domestic workers hasn't been growing faster than Singapore's reliance on foreign labour generally; it's been growing in step with it, year after year, through a financial crisis, a pandemic, and every cooling and loosening of immigration policy in between. Even the COVID-19 border closures barely dented the count — 247,400 in 2020 to 246,300 in 2021 — because most of the workers already here simply stayed, even as new arrivals slowed to a trickle.

By 2010, roughly one in every five Singaporean households employed a foreign domestic worker — up from about one in 13 just two decades earlier. That ratio has essentially held since, even as the raw numbers kept climbing, which points to why: it's not that more Singaporeans are choosing to hire help so much as more households now need it, particularly as more women work full-time and the population ages. The [subsidised eldercare infrastructure covered elsewhere on this blog]({{ '/2026/07/18/a-typical-day-for-singapores-silver-generation/' | relative_url }}) exists alongside, not instead of, hundreds of thousands of live-in workers doing that care privately, one household at a time.

**Why it matters today:** the amah generation is gone, but the gap they filled never closed — it was filled instead by an entire, largely invisible workforce whose numbers now rival a small city's, doing work that keeps a great many Singaporean households running without ever quite being counted as part of the story.
