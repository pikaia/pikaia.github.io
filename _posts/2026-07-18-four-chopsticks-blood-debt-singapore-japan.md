Commuters passing Esplanade MRT station walk beneath four tapering white columns that locals have nicknamed the "four chopsticks" for as long as most of them can remember, without necessarily knowing what the monument actually marks. Underneath it are the remains of more than 600 people, recovered from mass graves found around the island in 1962 — victims of a massacre that Singapore and Japan spent the following decades finding an uneasy but lasting way to live with.

*The Civilian War Memorial, Beach Road — four 67-metre columns representing Singapore's Chinese, Malay, Indian and Eurasian communities, built over the remains of Sook Ching victims. (Photo: Bijay Chaurasia / Wikimedia Commons, CC BY-SA 4.0)*

The massacre itself is known as Operation Sook Ching, carried out between 18 February and 4 March 1942, in the weeks immediately after Singapore fell. Japanese forces screened the male Chinese population for "anti-Japanese elements" and executed those they judged a threat, along with many who simply drew an unlucky mark from a screening officer. The death toll has never been settled: Japan's own wartime figure was 6,000; Singapore and Malayan estimates have ranged from 25,000 to as high as 100,000. Lee Kuan Yew, who narrowly avoided being taken away himself, later put the verifiable figure at around 70,000 across Singapore and Malaya combined.

For twenty years the matter sat largely unresolved. Then in 1962, construction work and land clearance began turning up mass graves — more than 40 sites, including a notorious cluster in Siglap that came to be called the "Valley of Death." Public anger followed quickly: on 25 August 1963, more than 100,000 people rallied at City Hall demanding Japan pay what protesters were by then calling a "blood debt." It took three more years of negotiation, but on 25 October 1966, Japan agreed to pay S$50 million — half as an outright grant, half as a loan. Part of that money built the Civilian War Memorial, unveiled by Prime Minister Lee Kuan Yew on 15 February 1967, the 25th anniversary of the surrender, with a wreath-laying ceremony still held there every year on that date.

What's easy to miss, looking back, is how deliberately pragmatic that settlement was. Lee said openly that pressing the "blood debt" claim risked scaring off investment from what was then the world's second-largest economy — and pursued it anyway, but as a one-time, closed settlement rather than an open wound. That distinction matters for what came after. In 2002, Singapore and Japan signed the Japan–Singapore Economic Partnership Agreement, Japan's first such agreement with any country. By the late 2010s Japan had become Singapore's third-largest foreign investor and Singapore had become Japan's largest in Southeast Asia; more than 5,300 Japanese companies now operate here, bilateral merchandise trade passed S$56 billion in 2025, and in March 2026 the two countries upgraded their ties to a formal Strategic Partnership, 60 years after diplomatic relations began.

Some of that postwar goodwill has flowed in less visible ways. Since 1959, a Japanese organisation called AOTS — originally the Association for Overseas Technical Scholarship, now the Association for Overseas Technical Cooperation and Sustainable Partnerships — has run technical and management training programmes bringing engineers, technicians and managers from across Asia to Japan, and sending Japanese experts the other way. As of March 2025 it had trained or otherwise reached almost 479,000 people cumulatively, with more than 19,000 going through its programmes in a typical year. It's a quiet, decades-long thread of the same postwar relationship, largely invisible next to trade figures and memorials but arguably just as consequential for the region.

Not every country that suffered under Japanese occupation settled things the way Singapore did. Japan normalised relations with South Korea in 1965 and with China in 1972, but without anything quite as final as Singapore's one-time payment and memorial. The comfort women issue remains a live grievance in South Korea. The Nanjing Massacre, with a death toll still disputed between roughly 40,000 and 300,000, continues to shape Chinese public sentiment toward Japan. Visits by Japanese leaders to Yasukuni Shrine — where 14 convicted Class-A war criminals were enshrined in 1978 alongside Japan's war dead — still trigger diplomatic protests whenever they happen. Economic ties between Japan and both countries have grown enormously since the 1980s, much as they have with Singapore, but the political relationship has never had an equivalent closing chapter.

**Why it matters today:** Singapore's peace with Japan wasn't the absence of a grievance — it was a grievance that got a number attached to it, a monument built, and a date fixed on the calendar, which turned out to be enough to let everything else move forward. Most people walking past the four chopsticks today have no reason to know that's what actually happened there.
