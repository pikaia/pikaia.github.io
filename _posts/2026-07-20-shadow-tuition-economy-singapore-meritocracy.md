The Ministry of Education has never made private tuition a requirement for anything. Officially, it doesn't exist as part of the system at all — no school asks whether a student attends it, no policy assumes it. And yet Singaporean households spent S$1.8 billion on it in 2023, more than seven in ten students now receive some form of it, and it has quietly become the single largest discretionary expense in a typical Singaporean family's education budget, ahead of enrichment classes, overseas trips, and everything else combined. A system built on the promise that merit alone decides who gets ahead has, almost by accident, let a shadow industry become one of the biggest levers on outcomes it never officially acknowledges.

The industry's growth traces a straight line. Singapore's Household Expenditure Survey put total tuition and enrichment spending at S$680 million in 2008; by 2013 that had grown to S$1.1 billion, by 2018 to S$1.4 billion, and by 2023 to S$1.8 billion — more than doubling in fifteen years, comfortably outpacing the economy it sits inside.

The money isn't spread evenly. Households in the top income bracket spend an average of S$162.60 a month on tuition; households in the bottom bracket spend S$36.30 — more than four times less. That gap is the whole story in miniature: a system that officially rewards effort and ability ends up, in practice, rewarding whichever families can afford to buy their children more shots at demonstrating that effort and ability. Tuition doesn't replace merit as the stated rule of the game. It just quietly decides who gets more chances to prove they have it.

*A personal note: I was part of the last cohort that could still get into a local university without actually passing a second language. Failing to pass wasn't going to stop me — but nobody told me that clearly enough at the time, and every cohort before mine had needed the pass, so I spent years in Mandarin tuition trying to clear a bar I wasn't sure I'd be held to. It didn't really work; my Mandarin never got much better for it. I got into a local university anyway. Looking back, most of that tuition spend wasn't really about getting ahead of anyone — it was insurance against a rule I didn't fully understand and couldn't afford to gamble on being wrong about.*

That's the part official messaging never quite captures. MOE has always been careful to describe tuition as optional, something families choose rather than something the system requires. But when a rule like a second-language pass requirement for university admission exists — and it still does today, just with more provisional-admission flexibility built in than there used to be — "optional" reads very differently to a family that can absorb the risk of skipping tuition than to one that can't. Opting out feels safe only if being wrong about the rules doesn't cost you very much.

*A personal note: I do genuinely wish I'd come out of all that with real Mandarin fluency — it's a skill I still want, not one I resent having been pushed toward. What I resented as a kid was how arbitrary the whole hurdle felt, tuition and all. If I ever move back to Singapore, using SkillsFuture credit to finally take proper Mandarin lessons, on my own terms and with none of the old institutional pressure attached, sounds like exactly the kind of thing that money should be for.*

<div style="clear: both;"></div>

**Where it fits in the bigger story:** Singapore's meritocracy was designed to measure ability; what it's actually measuring, increasingly, is which families can afford to buy their children more attempts at demonstrating it — a shadow economy the official system has never had to answer for, because on paper, it was never supposed to exist.
